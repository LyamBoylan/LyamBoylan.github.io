Idea

Arithmetic indicators are elementary functions that simulate indicator functions.[1][1] This article uses Iverson brackets, $[\ \cdot\ ]$, to notate indicator functions. I.e; for any proposition, $\phi$, we have $$[\phi]=\begin{cases}1 &: \phi,\\0 &: \neg\phi.\end{cases}$$

List of arithmetic indicators

In combination with eachother, arithmetic indicators can perform a wide variety of operations. They are usually computationally inefficient, however their utility is in their novelty, ability to have their existence stand as explicit examples / counter-examples, and for the allowance of turing-completeness of a system which is limited to elementary operations, like most calculators.

Logic

  • $[\top]=1$.
  • $[\bot]=0$.
  • $[p \wedge q]=[p][q]$.
  • $[\neg p]=1-[p]$.
  • $[p\vee q]=[p]+[q]-[p][q]$.

Equalities and inequalities

  • $[a=b]=\left\lfloor\frac{1}{1+|a-b|}\right\rfloor$ .
    • Alternatively, $[a=b]=0^{|a-b|}$ if $0^0:=1$,
    • or $[a=b]=\left\lfloor(1+\epsilon)^{-|a-b|}\right\rfloor$ (for any $\epsilon>0$)..
  • $[a\leq b]=\left\lfloor\frac{2}{\pi}\cot^{-1}(a-b)\right\rfloor$.
    • Alternatively, $[a\leq b]=\left\lfloor\frac{2}{1+(1+\epsilon)^{a-b}}\right\rfloor$ (for any $\epsilon>0$).
  • $[a > b]=\left\lceil\frac{2}{\pi}\tan^{-1}(a-b)\right\rceil$.

Number theory

  • $[p\text{ is prime}]=[\sigma_0(p)=2]$, where $\sigma_0$ is the divisor function.
